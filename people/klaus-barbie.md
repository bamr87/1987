# Klaus Barbie — Convicted of Crimes Against Humanity (July 4, 1987)

**Category**: People
**Key figures**: Klaus Barbie (defendant), Jacques Vergès (defense attorney), Beate Klarsfeld (Nazi hunter who located Barbie), Sabine Zlatin (founder of the Izieu children's home), Lea Feldblum (sole survivor of the Izieu deportation), Judge André Cerdini (presiding)

## Summary

On July 4, 1987, a French court in Lyon convicted Klaus Barbie of crimes against humanity and sentenced him to life imprisonment — the maximum penalty under French law and the first such conviction in French legal history.

Klaus Barbie (born October 25, 1913) served as the head of the Gestapo in Lyon from November 1942 to August 1944, earning the sobriquet "the Butcher of Lyon." During his tenure he was responsible for approximately 4,000 deaths and 7,500 deportations. His most notorious acts included the torture and execution of Jean Moulin — the leading figure of the French Resistance and General de Gaulle's chief representative in occupied France — and the roundup and deportation of 44 Jewish children (aged 3–13) and seven adults from the Izieu children's home on April 6, 1944. The children were transported to Auschwitz and murdered there. Barbie also organized a last-minute convoy of 650 prisoners on August 11, 1944 — hours before Lyon's liberation — the majority of whom perished.

After Germany's defeat, Barbie evaded capture by working as a counterintelligence agent for U.S. Army intelligence from 1947 to 1951, during which time the Americans helped exfiltrate him and his family to Bolivia via a "ratline" escape network. In Bolivia he lived for two decades under the alias "Klaus Altmann," working as a livestock merchant and later as an intelligence operative for the Bolivian government. Nazi hunter Beate Klarsfeld identified him in 1972; after years of diplomatic pressure, Bolivia extradited him to France in February 1983.

The trial began on May 11, 1987, in the Criminal Court of the Rhône in Lyon. Because French law imposed a 20-year statute of limitations on war crimes, Barbie could only be tried for crimes against humanity — acts defined under the Nuremberg principles as persecution of civilians for racial, political, or religious reasons, for which no limitation period applied. He faced 177 counts. Defense attorney Jacques Vergès, a combative and controversial figure, pursued a strategy of *rupture* (rupture defense), arguing that French colonialism in Algeria made France unfit to sit in judgment. The court rejected this approach. Barbie, present for most of the trial, remained unrepentant and expressed pride in his service to the Nazi regime.

The jury deliberated and returned a unanimous verdict of guilty on all charges on July 4 — symbolically, the United States Independence Day and the 11th anniversary of the Israeli Entebbe raid rescue. Barbie was sentenced to life imprisonment. He died of leukemia at Saint-Joseph Prison in Lyon on September 25, 1991, at the age of 77, never having shown remorse.

## Significance

The Barbie trial was France's first criminal prosecution explicitly for "crimes against humanity," a category that the Nuremberg Tribunals had introduced to international law in 1945 but that no French court had previously applied to a named defendant. By convicting Barbie specifically for acts against Jewish civilians and Resistance fighters as such — rather than under the broader category of war crimes — the trial clarified and extended the Nuremberg legal framework within French domestic law and set a precedent for subsequent French prosecutions of Paul Touvier (1994) and Maurice Papon (1998).

The proceedings forced an uncomfortable national reckoning. The revelation that U.S. intelligence had sheltered Barbie in exchange for anti-communist work, and Vergès's invocation of French colonial atrocities, complicated the simple narrative of France as a nation of resisters. Survivors' testimony — including that of 69-year-old Lea Feldblum, the sole adult survivor of the Izieu deportation — returned the children of Izieu to public memory and catalyzed the construction of the Mémorial de la Shoah at Izieu, which opened in 1994.

Internationally, the trial reinforced the principle that there is no statute of limitations for crimes against humanity, a norm subsequently embedded in the 1998 Rome Statute establishing the International Criminal Court.
