# Gary Hart's 1987 Presidential Campaign

**Category**: People
**Key figures**: Gary Hart (Senator, D-CO), Donna Rice, Lee Hart (Hart's wife), The Miami Herald investigative team

## Summary

Senator Gary Hart of Colorado was the clear frontrunner for the 1988 Democratic presidential nomination when he formally declared his candidacy on April 13, 1987, following frontrunner Mario Cuomo's decision not to run. A two-term senator and former presidential candidate in 1984, Hart led national polls by wide margins, and many observers considered him the presumptive Democratic nominee. His campaign collapsed within weeks of its launch under the weight of a sexual scandal that became a turning point in American political journalism.

In late April 1987, the *Miami Herald* received a tip that Hart was conducting an extramarital affair. Reporters staked out his Washington, D.C., townhouse and observed model and actress Donna Rice at the residence over a weekend. The story broke on May 3, and photographs subsequently surfaced showing Rice sitting in Hart's lap aboard the luxury yacht *Monkey Business* during an earlier Bimini trip — Hart wearing a "Monkey Business Crew" T-shirt. The images proved impossible to survive politically, particularly after Hart had publicly challenged journalists just days before the stakeout: "Follow me around. I'm serious. If anyone wants to put a tail on me, go ahead. They'd be very bored." On May 8, 1987, with the *Washington Post* poised to publish details of a separate alleged affair, Hart suspended his presidential campaign.

Hart re-entered the race on December 15, 1987, declaring "Let the people decide!" on the steps of New Hampshire's statehouse, but the comeback gained little traction. He received approximately four percent of the New Hampshire primary vote and withdrew permanently after poor Super Tuesday results in March 1988. Michael Dukakis became the Democratic nominee and lost the general election to Vice President George H.W. Bush.

## Significance

The Gary Hart scandal of 1987 is widely regarded as a watershed moment in American political journalism, marking the point at which the mainstream press began treating candidates' personal lives as legitimate subjects of public scrutiny. Before the *Herald* stakeout, an informal norm had kept candidates' extramarital conduct largely off-limits for major newspapers; the decision to surveil Hart's residence represented a permanent break with that convention. The episode crystallized debates about the boundary between public accountability and private life that had no settled answer before 1987 — and that continue to shape U.S. political coverage.

The scandal had concrete downstream effects on the 1988 election and beyond. Hart's withdrawal restructured the Democratic primary field entirely, elevating Dukakis and shaping the path to the Bush presidency. In the longer term, the Hart affair established investigative personal-conduct vetting as a standard tool of presidential campaigns and the press corps, and it intensified the use of opposition research to surface compromising information about candidates. Hart himself later argued that the *Herald*'s decision redefined the terms of public service in ways that deterred capable people from seeking office — a critique that remained part of American political discourse for decades.
