# 1987 World Series

**Category**: Society & Economics
**Key figures**: Frank Viola (World Series MVP), Tom Kelly (Twins manager), Whitey Herzog (Cardinals manager), Dan Gladden, Kent Hrbek, Kirby Puckett

## Summary

The 1987 World Series, played October 17–25, saw the Minnesota Twins defeat the St. Louis Cardinals four games to three, claiming the franchise's first World Series championship. It was also the first World Series ever played entirely indoors: all four Minnesota home games took place in the Hubert H. Humphrey Metrodome in Minneapolis, where crowd noise reached 125 decibels — the loudest ever recorded at a World Series game. The home team won every one of the seven contests, a pattern unique in Series history.

The Twins entered the postseason as surprise contenders, posting a .525 regular-season winning percentage (85–77), the lowest ever for a World Series champion at the time. In the Series itself the Twins dominated at the Metrodome — winning 10–1, 8–4, 11–5, and 4–2 in Games 1, 2, 6, and 7 respectively — while the Cardinals took all three road games at Busch Memorial Stadium in St. Louis. Key offensive moments included Dan Gladden's fourth-inning grand slam in Game 1 and Kent Hrbek's two-out grand slam in the pivotal Game 6 that sealed Minnesota's path to the deciding seventh game. Kirby Puckett hit .357 across the Series as the Twins' offensive catalyst.

Twins pitcher Frank Viola won the World Series MVP Award after going 2–1 across three starts with a 3.72 ERA over 19.1 innings pitched. The "Homer Hankies" — white handkerchiefs waved by Twins fans throughout the postseason — became an enduring symbol of the championship. Total Series attendance was 387,138 across seven games, averaging 55,305 per game.

## Significance

The 1987 World Series established several historic firsts. It was the first championship ever played entirely inside a domed stadium, reflecting the growing role of enclosed ballparks in Major League Baseball and raising lasting debates about how artificial playing surfaces and crowd acoustics affect competition. The symmetrical home-team dominance — neither club won a single road game across all seven contests — made it one of the most distinctive Series in the sport's history.

The Twins' victory defied the expectations set by their modest regular-season record, demonstrating that October baseball could produce decisive upsets regardless of standings. Their .525 winning percentage remained a record for the lowest by a World Series champion until the 2006 St. Louis Cardinals (.516) eclipsed it nearly two decades later. The series also marked the last World Series to feature a day game (Game 6 started at 3:00 p.m. CT), as subsequent television contracts moved all postseason broadcasts to prime-time evening scheduling. For Minnesota, the championship anchored a lasting regional sports identity built around the Metrodome era.
