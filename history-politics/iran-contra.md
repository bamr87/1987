# Iran-Contra Affair (1986–1987)

**Category**: History & Politics
**Key figures**: Ronald Reagan (President), Oliver North (NSC aide), John Poindexter (National Security Advisor), Robert McFarlane (former National Security Adviser), Caspar Weinberger (Defense Secretary), Fawn Hall (North's secretary), Lawrence Walsh (Independent Counsel)

## Summary

The Iran-Contra affair was the most damaging political scandal of Ronald Reagan's presidency, involving two covert operations that violated both law and congressional restrictions. The administration secretly sold weapons to Iran — a state designated a sponsor of terrorism and subject to a US arms embargo — in the hope of securing the release of American hostages held in Lebanon. Proceeds from the arms sales were then illegally diverted to fund Nicaraguan Contra rebels, circumventing the Boland Amendment (1984), which explicitly prohibited direct or indirect US military aid to the Contras.

Arms shipments to Iran began in August 1985 with a transfer of 96 TOW anti-tank missiles. NSC aide Lieutenant Colonel Oliver North, operating with the approval of National Security Advisor John Poindexter but without formal presidential authorization, orchestrated the diversion of approximately $48 million in arms-sale proceeds to the Contras. On November 3, 1986, the Lebanese magazine *Ash-Shiraa* exposed the arrangement. Reagan appeared on national television on November 13 acknowledging weapons transfers to Iran, and on November 25 Attorney General Edwin Meese publicly revealed that funds had been diverted to the Contras.

The Tower Commission — a special review board established on November 26, 1986, by Reagan himself — delivered its report on February 26, 1987, criticizing the administration's management style and confirming the arms-for-hostages character of the policy. On March 4, 1987, Reagan addressed the nation in a televised speech accepting responsibility. Joint televised congressional hearings before the Senate and House Iran-Contra committees ran from early May through early August 1987, heard testimony from more than 500 witnesses, and became the most-watched congressional hearings since the Watergate proceedings of 1973. Oliver North testified for six days beginning July 7, 1987, drawing an estimated 55 million viewers on his first day; dressed in his Marine uniform and decorated with medals, he defiantly defended his actions as patriotic while admitting he had misled Congress.

North and Poindexter were both convicted on multiple felony counts, but their convictions were later overturned on appeal on Fifth Amendment grounds related to their immunized congressional testimony. The only figure to serve prison time was Thomas G. Clines, sentenced to sixteen months. On December 24, 1992, outgoing President George H.W. Bush pardoned six officials involved, including Weinberger and McFarlane. Independent Counsel Lawrence Walsh, who concluded his investigation in 1993, characterized the affair as a sustained pattern of "deception and obstruction" by senior officials.

## Significance

The Iran-Contra affair raised fundamental constitutional questions about the balance of power between the executive branch and Congress in foreign policy. Reagan's approval rating fell from 67 percent to 46 percent — the steepest single drop for a sitting president since polling began — and permanently damaged his second-term credibility. The majority report signed by the joint congressional committee found that senior administration officials had "knowingly misled Congress," while the minority report, signed by Republican members, characterized the affair as executive overreach correctable through legislation rather than scandal.

Internationally, the affair undermined US credibility on counterterrorism policy and complicated relations with both Iran and the Contra movement. The revelation that the government secretly traded arms for hostages directly contradicted Reagan's publicly stated policy of never negotiating with terrorists or terrorist-supporting states. The hearings made Oliver North a polarizing national figure — simultaneously reviled as a law-breaker and celebrated as a patriot by different segments of the public — and cemented the affair as a defining event in debates about covert operations, executive accountability, and congressional oversight.
