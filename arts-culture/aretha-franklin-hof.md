# Aretha Franklin: First Woman in the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame

**Category**: Arts & Culture
**Key figures**: Aretha Franklin (inductee), Keith Richards (presenter), Cecil Franklin (brother; accepted award), Clive Davis (record executive; accepted award)

## Summary

On 21 January 1987, Aretha Louise Franklin became the first woman inducted into the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame, at the organization's second annual induction ceremony held at the Waldorf-Astoria Hotel in New York City. Keith Richards of The Rolling Stones presented the honor, describing Franklin as "the first lady to be inducted into this hall of fame." Franklin did not attend — reportedly due to a fear of flying — and her brother Cecil Franklin and record executive Clive Davis accepted the award on her behalf.

The Rock and Roll Hall of Fame was founded in 1983, and its inaugural induction ceremony on 23 January 1986 honored ten performers — among them Chuck Berry, Elvis Presley, Ray Charles, and Little Richard — without including a single woman. Criticism of the omission was swift, and the 1987 class addressed it by inducting Franklin among fifteen performers: Marvin Gaye, Carl Perkins, Roy Orbison, Muddy Waters, Bo Diddley, B.B. King, Smokey Robinson, Ricky Nelson, The Coasters, Eddie Cochran, Bill Haley, Jackie Wilson, Big Joe Turner, and Clyde McPhatter.

Aretha Franklin was born on 25 March 1942 in Memphis, Tennessee, and raised in Detroit, Michigan. The daughter of the celebrated Baptist minister Reverend C.L. Franklin, she developed her exceptional vocal range in church choirs before recording professionally as a teenager. After years of moderate success at Columbia Records, she signed with Atlantic Records in 1966 and immediately broke through: her 1967 recording of "Respect" — rearranged from an Otis Redding composition — became one of the most recognizable songs in popular music history and a defining anthem of both the civil rights and women's movements. By the time of her Hall of Fame induction she had accumulated dozens of top-forty R&B hits, won eleven consecutive Grammy Awards for Best Female R&B Vocal Performance (1968–1978), and was universally known as the "Queen of Soul." She went on to win 18 competitive Grammy Awards over her career.

## Significance

Franklin's 1987 induction was the first institutional acknowledgement by the Rock and Roll Hall of Fame that rock and roll's founding generation included women of foundational importance. Her selection recognized an artist who had not merely influenced the genre from its margins but had defined its emotional and spiritual core — bridging gospel, rhythm and blues, and pop in a manner that shaped virtually every subsequent female vocalist in popular music.

The milestone also drew sustained attention to structural gender disparities in music's most prestigious honors. By the late 2010s, women still comprised fewer than nine percent of all Hall of Fame inductees, a statistic that continued to fuel advocacy for more equitable selection processes. Franklin's 1987 induction opened a path eventually followed by Joni Mitchell (1997), Bonnie Raitt (2000), Stevie Nicks (solo, 2019 — the first woman inducted twice), and dozens of others. It stands as the reference point in the larger history of women's formal recognition in popular music.
