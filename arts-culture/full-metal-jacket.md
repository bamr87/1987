# Full Metal Jacket

**Category**: Arts & Culture
**Key figures**: Stanley Kubrick (director, producer, co-writer), Matthew Modine (Pvt. "Joker" Davis), R. Lee Ermey (Gunnery Sgt. Hartman), Vincent D'Onofrio (Pvt. "Pyle" Lawrence), Michael Herr and Gustav Hasford (co-screenwriters)

## Summary

*Full Metal Jacket* is a 1987 American war film directed and produced by Stanley Kubrick, released in the United States by Warner Bros. on June 26, 1987. Kubrick wrote the screenplay with Michael Herr and Gustav Hasford, adapting Hasford's 1979 autobiographical novel *The Short-Timers*. The film follows U.S. Marines through the Vietnam War era and is told in two distinct parts.

The first half is set at the Marine Corps Recruit Depot on Parris Island, where recruits — among them Private "Joker" Davis (Matthew Modine) and the overweight, struggling Private "Pyle" Lawrence (Vincent D'Onofrio) — endure brutal basic training under the relentless Gunnery Sergeant Hartman (R. Lee Ermey). The second half shifts to Vietnam in 1968, depicting urban combat during the Battle of Huế amid the Tet Offensive, as Joker, now a combat correspondent, experiences the disorientation and violence of war. The cast also includes Adam Baldwin, Arliss Howard, and Dorian Harewood. D'Onofrio reportedly gained about 70 pounds for his role, and R. Lee Ermey, a former real-life Marine drill instructor, contributed extensively to the dialogue of his character.

The film was a critical and commercial success, grossing roughly $120 million worldwide. It received an Academy Award nomination for Best Adapted Screenplay (Kubrick, Herr, and Hasford) and a Golden Globe nomination for R. Lee Ermey as Best Supporting Actor, among other honors. Ermey won the Boston Society of Film Critics Award for Best Supporting Actor, while Kubrick was named Best Director by the same organization.

## Significance

*Full Metal Jacket* is regarded as one of the defining films of Stanley Kubrick's later career and a landmark entry in the cinema of the Vietnam War. Its release placed it within a notable wave of Vietnam-themed Hollywood films of the mid-1980s, arriving the same year that Oliver Stone's *Platoon* won the Academy Award for Best Picture and shortly after *Hamburger Hill*. The film is especially noted for its unflinching first act, which dramatizes the dehumanizing process by which recruits are conditioned to become combat soldiers — a sequence widely cited for R. Lee Ermey's performance and for shaping popular perceptions of military boot camp.

The film's two-part structure, austere visual style, and ambivalent moral stance on warfare reinforced Kubrick's reputation for thematically rigorous, formally precise filmmaking. It remains a frequent subject of critical analysis and is consistently ranked among the most significant war films of its decade.
